# Depictive Adjectives (Complements or Adjuncts)

It describes the state of the subject/object at the time the action happened. It is related to the state of being of the qualified noun.

- Depictive Adjuncts

  - He licked the plate angry

    *licked* is a **Monotransitive Verb**, and *angry* is a **Depictive Adjunct** that refers to the subject.


  - I ate the pizza cold.

    *ate* is a **Monotransitive Verb**, and *cold* is a **Depictive Adjunct** that refers to the object.

- Depictive Complements

  - I found the walls yellow

    If you mean "I judged them", then *found* is a **Complex Transitive Verb** and *yellow* is an **Object Depictive Complement**

There are 2 types:

1. Physical Depictive
2. Evaluative Depictive

# Resultative Adjectives

In a sentence, they can be optional or mandatory.
When they're optional, they are called **Resultative Adjuncts**. When they're mandatory, they are called **Object Complements**.

When they go in a **Complex Transitive Structure**, they are **Object Complements**.

- He licked the plate clean.

  *licked* is a CTV because the adjective *clean* adds an ending to the action, so the action is not performed endlessly.
  Therefore, *clean* is a **Resultative Complement (Object Complement)**.

- He painted the door red.

  *painted* is a CTV because the adjective *red* specifies the actual change. Therefore, *red* is a **Resultative Complement**.

Resultative Adjectives can refer to the subject with **Unaccusative Verbs**. In this structure, Resultatives can be **Adjuncts** or **Complements**.

- The food turned bad. 
  - *turned* is an **Unaccusative Verb** and *bad* is a **Resultative Complement**.
- The river froze solid.
  - *froze* is an **Unaccusative Verb** and *solid* is a **Resultative Adjunct**

Sometimes, we can express a result with **Intransitive Verbs**. To do this, we have to use a *fake* (reflexive) object.

- He ran tired

  *tired* is not a Resultative here, it is a Depictive. If I want to express a result, English uses **Reflexive Pronouns** as a *fake object*. This allows the Resultative to refer to the  subject through the object.

- He ran himself tired.

  He is tired because of the running.

- The baby cried himself to sleep.

  The baby is now asleep because of the crying.

# Evaluative Adjectives

It expresses the speaker's subjective opinion or mental representation of the object. It is related to the state of mind of the person evaluating the noun.

Unlike a Resultative (which shows a physical change, like in "I painted it red"), a judgement shows a mental *label* being applied.

These can refer to the subject in a **Copular (Linking) structure** or a **Depictive Structure**, no longer being part of a Complex Transitive Structure.

Evaluative Adjectives are almost always Stative.

- Stative: He is intelligent (a stable quality/opinion)
- Dynamic: He is being rude (a temporary behavior).

<u>Note</u>: Most CTVs of judgement (*find, consider, deem*) only work with Stative evaluative adjectives because you are judging the *essence* or *state* of the person/thing rather than judging temporal acts.

Evaluative adjectives don't go with Unaccusative Verbs.

In Passive Voice Structures, its role is a **Subject Complement**:

- *The movie was found boring*. *boring* is a Subject Complement and an Evaluative Adjective.
- The house was painted red. *red* is a Resultative Subject Complement.

# Attributive Adjectives vs Predicate Adjectives

- Attributive Adjectives come before the noun (*the **interesting** book*)
- Predicate Adjectives come after a verb and relates back to a noun (*The book is **interesting***)

# Postpositive Adjectives

They are adjectives that are placed immediately after the noun they modify.
They aren't the standard way to speak today; they usually appear in English due to 3 specific reasons:

1. Fixed Phrases and Legal/Official Titles

   Examples: *Attorney general, court martial, heir apparent, poet laureate, notary public*.

2. Indefinite Pronouns

   When using words—like something, anything, someone, or nobody—, the adjective must go after the noun

   Examples: I want to tell you something *important*. (You cannot say important something) 

3. Adjectives with *Extra Weight* (Adjective Phrases)

   If an adjective is part of a longer phrase, we often move the whole thing behind the noun to prevent the sentence from becoming "top-heavy"

   Examples:

   - Standard: The available rooms
   - Postpositive: The rooms available for tonight.

# Stative Adjectives vs Dynamic Adjectives

- Stative Adjectives: Describe a quality that is perceived as stable or a "state of being"
- Dynamic Adjectives: Describe a behavior someone can *act out* (e.g. He is being brave). You cannot "*be interesting*" as a temporary act in the same way; it is treated as an inherent or perceived state.

# Classification of Adjectives

- Exclusive: Object Complement and Depictive Adjectives
  - Object Complement
    - Evaluative
    - Resultative
  - Depictive Adjectives
    - Physical
    - Evaluative

# English Adjective-Verb Relationships

* The term "adjective-verb relationships" isn't usually a single chapter title in a basic grammar book, but in advanced linguistics and syntax, it is a massive and fascinating field of study. It explores how adjectives and verbs "handshake" to create meaning.
* If you want to dive deeper into this, you are looking for the intersection of Syntax (sentence structure) and Semantics (meaning).
