# Evaluative Adjectives (Adjectives of Value/Opinion)

An **evaluative adjective** expresses the speaker’s subjective opinion, mental representation, or judgment of a noun, rather than an inherent physical property.

Unlike a **Resultative** (which denotes a physical change of state), an evaluative adjective applies a **mental label**.

## 1. Syntactic Structures

Evaluative adjectives are highly flexible and appear in three primary structures:

### A. Copular / Linking Structures (Subject Complements)

They easily attribute a subjective quality directly to the subject.

- *Example:* "He is **intelligent**." / "The meal was **delicious**."

### B. Complex Transitive Structures (Object Complements)

> ⚠️ **Correction to original note:** Evaluative adjectives frequently appear here. Verbs of mental judgment (*consider, deem, find, judge*) require an evaluative adjective to act as an **Object Complement**.

- *Example:* "I consider him **foolish**."
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *consider* is a Complex Transitive Verb, *him* is the Direct Object, and *foolish* is the Evaluative Object Complement.

### C. Depictive Structures (Adjuncts)

They can describe the psychological state or evaluative status of a noun during an event.

- *Example:* "They married **young**." / "She arrived at the party **radiant**."

## 2. The Stative vs. Dynamic Distinction

Evaluative adjectives are predominantly **stative** (representing stable qualities or enduring opinions) rather than **dynamic** (temporary behaviors).

- **Stative:** *He is intelligent.* (A stable quality/opinion).
- **Dynamic:** *He is being rude.* (A temporary, deliberate behavior).

> 💡 **CTV Constraint:** Complex Transitive Verbs of judgment (*find, consider, deem*) generally **only** accept stative evaluative readings because you are judging the essence/state of the entity, not a temporary act.
>
> - *Correct:* "I deem him **trustworthy**." (Stative quality)
> - *Incorrect/Awkward:* "I deem him being rude."

## 3. Interaction with Unaccusative Verbs

Evaluative adjectives **do not** typically occur as resultatives with unaccusative verbs. Unaccusative verbs (*freeze, break, melt*) inherently involve a spontaneous or physical change of state, which conflicts with a purely subjective mental evaluation.

- *Incorrect:* "The vase broke beautiful."

## 4. Passive Voice Transformations

When a complex transitive sentence is passivized, the **Object Complement** shifts to become a **Subject Complement**. Your analysis here was perfectly spot on!

- **Active:** "The critic found the movie **boring**." (*boring* = Object Complement)
- **Passive:** "The movie was found **boring**." (*boring* = Subject Complement / Evaluative)
- **Active:** "They painted the house **red**." (*red* = Resultative Object Complement)
- **Passive:** "The house was painted **red**." (*red* = Resultative Subject Complement)
