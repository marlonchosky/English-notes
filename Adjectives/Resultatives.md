## 1. Transitive Resultatives (Adjuncts vs. Complements)

In transitive structures, resultatives typically modify the **direct object**. Syntacticians debate whether they are always complements or can be adjuncts, but a great rule of thumb is looking at whether the verb *requires* the result to make sense in that context.

### Resultative Complements (with Complex Transitive Verbs)

When a verb requires the resultative to complete its intended meaning, it behaves as a **Complex Transitive Verb**.

- **Example 1:** *He licked the plate **clean**.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *licked* acts as a **Complex Transitive Verb** here. The adjective *clean* denotes the final bounded state of the object (*the plate*) resulting from the licking.
- **Example 2:** *He painted the door **red**.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *painted* is a **Complex Transitive Verb**, and *red* is the **Resultative Object Complement**. Without *red*, "He painted the door" is grammatical, but *red* specifies the resulting state change of the object.

## 2. Intransitive Resultatives (Unaccusative Verbs)

Resultatives can refer to the **subject**, but generally *only* if the verb is **Unaccusative** (an intransitive verb where the subject undergoes a change of state, acting structurally like an underlying object).

### Unaccusative Complements vs. Adjuncts

- **Example 1 (Complement):** *The food turned **bad**.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *turned* is a **Copular/Linking Verb** (closely tied to unaccusatives). Because "turned" requires a predicate to complete its meaning (*The food turned* is incomplete), *bad* is a **Subject Complement**.
- **Example 2 (Adjunct):** *The river froze **solid**.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *froze* is a true **Unaccusative Verb**. *Solid* is a **Resultative Adjunct** because "The river froze" is a complete, grammatical sentence on its own. *Solid* simply adds the resulting state.

## 3. Intransitive Verbs & "Fake" (Reflexive) Objects

Purely unergative intransitive verbs (like *run, laugh, cry*) cannot take a resultative directly because the subject is actively performing the action, not receiving a state change. To force a resultative meaning, English uses a **"Fake" (often Reflexive) Object**.

- **Incorrect (Depictive):** *He ran **tired**.* * *Note:* As you correctly noted, this means he *was* tired while running (Depictive). It does not mean running made him tired.
- **Correct (Resultative):** *He ran **himself** **tired**.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *himself* acts as a **Fake Object**. This structural trick allows the resultative *tired* to modify the object, which inherently bounces the result back to the subject.
- **Prepositional Resultative:** *The baby cried **himself** to sleep.*
  - **Grammar Breakdown:** *himself* is the fake object, and the prepositional phrase *to sleep* functions resultatively.

### 💡 Notebook Expansion: The "Into" Transition Rule

When a resultative state represents a radical change in physical form, shape, or division, English frequently uses a **Prepositional Phrase** starting with **"into"** instead of a pure adjective.

- **The Logic:** As you noted, "into" inherently denotes **movement, transformation, or transition** from one state of being to another.
- **Structure:** `[Subject] + [Verb] + [Resultative PP]`
- **Examples:**
  - *The vase broke **into pieces**.* (Resulting state: it is now in pieces) 
  - *The ice melted **into water**.* (Resulting state: it is now liquid)
  - The food turned into poison (turned here is a CIV)
  - *The caterpillar turned **into a butterfly**.* (Resulting state: completely transformed)



**Unaccusative Transition:** $\text{The vase broke } [\text{into pieces}]_{\text{(Subject Resultative PP Complement)}}$

**Unergative Transition:** $\text{He cried } [\text{himself}]_{\text{(DO)}} \ [\text{to sleep / into a frenzy}]_{\text{(Object Resultative PP Complement)}}$
