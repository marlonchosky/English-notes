# Description

It is the rule that allows a verb to agree with the **meaning** (the notion) of a subject rather than just its literal **grammatical form**.

# The "Look-Through" Mechanism

Words like *all, some, half or none* don't have a fixed number. Their *notion* depends on the noun they are quantifying.

| Sentence                 | Literal Head  | Notional Subject | Verb Choice     |
| ------------------------ | ------------- | ---------------- | --------------- |
| All of the **water**...  | All (Pronoun) | Singular mass    | ...**is** gone  |
| All of the **miners**... | All (Pronoun) | Plural group     | ...**are** safe |

# Notional Concord Without an "of-phrase"

The reason this can be confusing is that the **result** is the same as the "*of-phrase*" version, but the **reason** is different.

- **With "of" (Notional)**: "All of water **is**..." → The verb looks through *All* to find *water*.
- **Without "of" (Formal)**: "All the water **is**..." → The verb simply agrees with the Head Noun *water*

In both cases, you use *is*.
The only difference is whether you are looking at the **Head** (water) or looking through a **Pronoun** (All) to find the noun.
