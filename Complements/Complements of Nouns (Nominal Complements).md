# Complements of Nouns (Nominal Complements)

A **Nominal Complement** is a clausal or prepositional phrase argument that is structurally selected by an abstract noun to complete its meaning. It answers the structural question: *"The [Noun] of what?"* or *"To do what?"*

Unlike optional adjective modifiers (e.g., *"The decision [from yesterday]"*), a complement completes the internal semantic definition of the noun itself.

---

## 1. Infinitival Clause Complements

This structure occurs frequently with abstract nouns of volition, choice, ability, or opportunity (*decision, plan, attempt, ability, permission*).

* *The decision **to leave** was hard.*
    * **decision:** Head Noun of the Subject Noun Phrase (NP).
    * **to leave:** Noun Complement (Non-Finite Infinitival Clause).
    * **Syntactic Control (PRO):** The non-finite clause contains an unexpressed subject ($\text{PRO}$). Rather than being completely generic, this "PRO" is bound by context to whoever is responsible for the decision: 
      $$\text{The decision } [\text{PRO}_i \text{ to leave}] \text{ was hard.}$$

---

## 2. Prepositional Phrase Complements

Many relational or nominalized action nouns require a fixed prepositional phrase argument to fulfill their lexical framework.

* *His fear **of spiders** controlled his choices.*
    * **fear:** Head Noun.
    * **of spiders:** Prepositional Noun Complement (PP completing the core target of the fear).
* *The destruction **of the city** took only hours.*
    * **destruction:** Nominalized Noun (derived from the transitive verb *destroy*).
    * **of the city:** Prepositional Noun Complement (behaving like the underlying direct object of the action).

---

## 3. Clausal Complements (Finite Content / That-Clauses)

Abstract nouns of cognition, fact, or announcement (*fact, rumor, idea, claim, news*) select finite clausal complements.

* *The rumor **that the server crashed** spread quickly.*
    * **rumor:** Head Noun.
    * **that the server crashed:** Clausal Noun Complement (Finite Content Clause).
    * **that:** **Complementizer** (Subordinating Conjunction) marking the structural boundary.

---

### 💡 Syntax Guardrail: Noun Complement vs. Relative Clause

It is easy to mistake a Clausal Noun Complement for a Relative Clause because they both start with "that". Use this structural diagnostic to tell them apart:

1. **The Gap Test:** A **Relative Clause** always has an empty structural gap ($\emptyset$) inside it because the relative pronoun has been moved. A **Noun Complement** is a complete content clause with no missing slots.
2. **The Substitution Test:** In a relative clause, "that" can be swapped with "which". In a noun complement, swapping "that" with "which" causes a total structural crash.

| Structure Type      | Sentence Example                                        | Breakdown                                                    |
| :------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Noun Complement** | *The rumor **that** the developer left.*                | Clause is complete (*the developer left*). Cannot say: *\*The rumor which the developer left.* |
| **Relative Clause** | *The rumor **that** the developer started $\emptyset$.* | Clause has a gap (*started [what?]*). Can say: *The rumor which the developer started.* |
