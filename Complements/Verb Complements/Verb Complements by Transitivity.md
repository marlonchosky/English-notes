# For Intransitive Verbs

## 1. Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause)

Completes a verb of choice/intention.

- I agreed **to attend**
  - agreed: **Intransitive Verb**, and it is the agent for "to attend"
  - to attend: **Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause)**

- I agreed **for him to attend**
  - agreed: **Intransitive Verb**
  - for him to attend: **Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause)**. *for* is just a **Complementizer** and *him* is the agent for "*to attend*"



## 2. Adverbial Complement (<span type="group-2">Complex Intransitive Verbs CIVs</span>)

Verbs are called **Complex Intransitive Verbs** or **Copular-Like**.

- The deposits <span type="group-2">reside</span> **underground**. 
  - underground: **Locative Complement** and **Adverb of Place**
- The shift <span type="group-2">lasted</span> **until midnight**.
  - until midnight: **Temporal Complement** and **Adverb of Time**



## 3. Prepositional Phrase/Complement (<span type="group-2">Prepositional Verb</span>)

Intransitive Verbs require a "hook" (the preposition to grab their object)

- I <span type="group-2">listened</span> **to the book**.
  - listened: **Intransitive and Prepositional Verb**
  - to the book: **Prepositional Phrase and Complement**
- I <span type="group-2">agreed</span> to it
  - agreed: **Prepositional Verb**
  - to it: **Prepositional Phrase and Complement**

==<u>Note</u>==: Some verbs like *agree, refuse and promise* are **"Infinitive-Only" verbs**. Even though they can take a PP with a noun, they **refuse** the gerund and insist on the infinitive for actions, so **<span type="incorrect">you cannot say</span>** "I agreed to attending". But there are some rare verbs where "**to**" is a preposition that accepts a gerund, like "**look forward to**", "**be used to**", "**object to**", "**commit to**" 



## 4. Clausal Complement (also called That-Clause, Noun Clauses, Content Clauses or Complement Clauses)

- I agreed **that I would attend**

  - that: It is optional. It is just a **Complementizer** or a **Subordinating Conjunction**. It doesn't stand in for a noun, it just signals the start of the clause.

  - agreed: **Intransitive**

# For Transitive Verbs

## 1. Direct Object (<span type="group-1">Monotransitive Verbs</span>) 

The entity **directly affected** or **acted upon**

- The workers <span type="group-1">extracted</span> the ore
  - extracted: **Monotransitive Verb**
  - the ore: **Object Complement**




## 2. Verbal Complement (<span type="group-1">CTVs</span>)

### Infinitive and Bare Infinitive (<span type="group-2">*DO → Action*</span>)

- He made me **clean my room**. (*Bare Infinitive Phrase*)
- I saw the sun **set** (*Bare Infinitive Phrase*)
- I want my father **to go** there (*Infinitive Phrase*)
- I get my brother **to clean my room** (*Infinitive Phrase*)



### Participle (Present/Past) (<span type="group-2">*DO ↔ Action*</span>)

- I saw the sun **setting** (*Present Participle*)
- I saw my room **cleaned** (*Past Participle and Passive Voice*)
- I had my room **cleaned** (*Past Participle and Passive voice*)



### Verbs of Perception

When you use a verb of **perception** (like *watch, see, hear, feel*) or certain verbs of **state** (like *keep, leave, catch*), the Object Complement often takes the form of a **Present Participle** or a **Bare Infinitive**.

- I watched the sun setting

  - *watched* is a **CTV**. Specifically, **a Verb of Perception**.
  - "*the sun*" is a **Direct Object** and **Noun Phrase**.
  - *setting* is the **Object Complement and a Present Participle** (*It describes the ongoing action of the object (the sun) at the moment you perceived it*)

- I watched the sun set

  - This is a Completed Action.

- I saw the room cleaned

  - It also can be a Past Participle Phrase. In this case, the relationship between the Object and the Complement becomes **Passive**.

  - It describes a completed, passive action that happened to the object. You didn't see *who* did it; you saw the *result* of the action on the room.

### Comparison

Be careful! Not all Complex Transitive Verbs are as flexible as *watch* or *see*.

- **Perception (Watch/See/Hear)**: Can take **Infinitives** AND **Present Participles** AND **Past Participles**.
- **Causative (Make, Have)**: Can take **Bare Infinitive** and **Past Participle**.



## 3. Adverbial Complement (<span type="group-1">CTVs</span>)

- They put the book **on the table**.
  - put: **CTV** (it has a **DO** and a **Locative Complement**). 
  - the book: **DO** and 
  - on the table: **Locative Complement**
- I timed the process **at three hours**
  - timed: **CTV**
  - at three hours: **Temporal Complement**

<u>==Note==</u>: An Adverbial Complement is <span type="incorrect">not</span> a type of Object Complement. Even though the Adverbial Complement can be after a DO as an Object Complement can, the Object Complement typically characterizes the object itself; meanwhile, the Adverbial Complement simply places that object in a physical context.



## 4. Prepositional Phrase/Complement (<span type="group-2">Ditransitive Prepositional Verb</span>)

- I provided him with the file

  - *provided* is a **Ditransitive Prepositional Verb**

  - *him* is the **DO**

  - "*with the file*" is the **Prepositional Complement**.

<u>==Note==</u>: The Prepositional Complement in this last sentence, where the DO is present, is <span type="incorrect">**not**</span> a type of **Object Complement**.



## 5. Object Complement (<span type="group-1">CTVs</span>)

The word or phrase that tells us *the state, or identity of the Direct Object*.

## 5.1. Adjective

It describes a state/result, or a mental label.

### Resultative Adjective (<span type="group-2">*DO = New State*</span>)

- He made the bread **brown**.

### Depictive Adjective (<span type="group-2">*DO + State*</span>)

- I found it **interesting**.
- I had my room **clean**.
  - You are often describing a **maintained state** or a **condition** you required. You aren't necessarily "cleaning" it in that moment; you are "having" it in that state.



## 5.2. Noun Phrase (<span type="group-2">*DO = Identity*</span>)

Describes an **identity/role**.

- They elected him **manager**.
