# Verb Complement

All parts that are mandatory for the verb.



## Direct Object

The entity **directly affected** or "**acted upon**"

- The workers extracted **the ore**. (*Monotransitive*)



## Locative Complement

The **location/position** (**Place Locative**) or **moment/time** (**Temporal Locative**) required by the verb.

### With Intransitive Verbs

When these verbs are intransitive and have a Locative Complement, they are also called **Complex Intransitive** Or **Copular-Like**:

- The deposits reside **underground**. (*Intransitive*)
  - *underground* is a **Place Locative Complement**.
- The shift lasted **until midnight**. (*Intransitive*)
  - "*until midnight*" is a  **Temporal Locative Complement** and an **Adverbial of Time**

### With Transitive Verbs

The **Locative Complement** can exist with **Transitive Verbs**:

- They put the book **on the table**.
  - *put* is a **CTV** (it has a **DO**). "*the book*" is the **DO** and "*on the table*" is a **Place Locative Complement**

<u>==Note==</u>: A Locative Complement is <span type="incorrect">not</span> a type of Object Complement. Even though the Locative Complement can be after a DO as an Object Complement can, the Object Complement typically characterizes the object itself; meanwhile, the Locative Complement simply places that object in a physical context.



## Non-Finite Infinitive Clause

Completes a verb of choice/intention.

- I agreed **to attend**.
  - *agreed* is an **Intransitive Verb**. The *agent* for "*to attend*" is "**I**" and "*to attend*" is an Infinitive Verb/Clause.

<u>Note</u>: It seems like the "to attend" part is an **Infinitive Phrase** rather than an **Infinitive Clause** because the verb is non-finite (it is not conjugated); however, it is highly recognized as a **Clause** because the Infinitive Verb can **be given its own agent**, like it is explained below.

### Special Case: Giving the Infinitive Verb its own agent

There are some verbs (like **agree**) that seem to have a preposition after (like **for**), but this preposition should <span type="incorrect">**NOT**</span> be analyzed as a **Prepositional Phrase**; instead, the preposition word is not an actual preposition but it is a **Complementizer** (a marker) introducing the **Infinitive Clause**, and also, allows the **Infinitive Verb** to have its own subject.

- I agreed **for him to attend**
  - *agreed* is an **Intransitive Verb**.
  - "*for him to attend*" is the **Verb Complement**, and an **Non-Finite Infinitive Clause**. "*for*" is just a **Complementizer** and the *agent* for the **Infinitive Verb** is "*him*"



## Prepositional Phrase (also called Prepositional Object or Prepositional Complement)

Some Intransitive Verbs require a "hook" (the preposition to grab their object)

- I listened **to the book**
  - Compare it with "I read **the book**", where the verb grabs their object directly. In both cases, "the book" is what you are interacting with, but the grammar uses a different "connector"
  - "*listened*" is an **Intransitive Verb**, also called a **Prepositional Verb**.
- I agreed **to it**
  - "*to it*" is the **PP**, and also a **Prepositional Complement or Prepositional Object**

==<u>Note</u>==: Some verbs like *agree, refuse and promise* are **"Infinitive-Only" verbs**. Even though they can take a PP with a noun, they **refuse** the gerund and insist on the infinitive for actions, so **<span type="incorrect">you cannot say</span>** "I agreed to attending". But there are some rare verbs where "**to**" is a preposition that accepts a gerund, like "**look forward to**", "**be used to**", "**object to**", "**commit to**" 



Sometimes, a **Transitive Verb** can have both a **DO and a Prepositional Complement**. This is called a **Ditransitive Prepositional Verb**. The **Prepositional Complement** is a third participant in the verb, it usually answers "What else is involved in this action?

- I provided him with the file
  - *provided* is a **Ditransitive Prepositional Verb**
  - *him* is the **DO**
  - "*with the file*" is the **Prepositional Complement**.

<u>==Note==</u>: The Prepositional Complement in this last sentence, where the DO is present, is <span type="incorrect">**not**</span> a type of **Object Complement**.



## That-Clauses (also called Noun Clauses or Content Clauses or Complement Clauses)

- I agreed **that I would attend**
  - *that* is optional. It is just a **Complementizer** or a **Subordinating Conjunction**. It doesn't stand in for a noun, it just signals the start of the clause.





## Special case: When the verb has a Direct Object

When a verb has a **DO**, then the verb can have another type of Verb Complement called **Object Complement**.

### Object Complement

The word or phrase that tells us *the state, identity, or action of the Direct Object*.

- They made the ore **pure**. (*Complex Transitive*)

An Object Complement slot can be filled by:

#### Adjective

It describes a state/result, or a mental label.

- He made the bread **brown**. (*brown is a **Resultative Adjective***)

- I found it **interesting**. (*interesting is a **Depictive Object Complement***)




#### Noun Phrase

Describes an **identity/role**.

- They elected him **manager**.



#### Verb Phrase

Describes a **potential action**. Even though the **relationship between the DO and OC isn't equal** in a literal sense, the Verb Phrase is still an OC because it describes an **attribute or action required of the Direct Object** to complete the verb's meaning. 

- He made me **clean my room**. (*Bare Infinitive*)
- I want my father **to go** there (*Infinitival Phrase*)
- I saw the sun **setting** (*Present Participle*)
- I saw the sun set (*Bare Infinitive*)
- I saw my room **cleaned** (*Past Participle*)

##### Verbs of Perception

When you use a verb of **perception** (like *watch, see, hear, feel*) or certain verbs of **state** (like *keep, leave, catch*), the Object Complement often takes the form of a **Present Participle** or a **Bare Infinitive**.

- I watched the sun setting

  - *watched* is a **CTV**. Specifically, **a Verb of Perception**.
  - "*the sun*" is a **Direct Object** and **Noun Phrase**.
  - *setting* is the **Object Complement and a Present Participle** (*It describes the ongoing action of the object (the sun) at the moment you perceived it*)
  
- I watched the sun set

  - This is a Completed Action.

- I saw the room cleaned

  - It also can be a Past Participle Phrase. In this case, the relationship between the Object and the Complement becomes **Passive**.
-  It describes a completed, passive action that happened to the object. You didn't see *who* did it; you saw the *result* of the action on the room.

##### Comparison

Be careful! Not all Complex Transitive Verbs are as flexible as *watch* or *see*.

- **Perception (Watch/See/Hear)**: Can take **Infinitives** AND **Present Participles** AND **Past Participles**.
- **Causative (Make, Have)**: Can take **Bare Infinitive** and **Past Participle**.
