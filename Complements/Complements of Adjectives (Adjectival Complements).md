# Complements of Adjectives (Adjectival Complements)

An **Adjective Complement** is a mandatory (or highly expected) structural partner that follows an adjective to complete its meaning. It answers the question **"in what respect?"**, **"of what?"**, or **"why?"** the adjective applies to the noun.

Unlike an optional adjunct, removing an adjective complement can make the sentence grammatically incomplete or alter the semantic intent of the adjective.

---

## 1. Prepositional Phrase Complements

Many adjectives require a specific prepositional "hook" to link to their complement noun phrase or gerund phrase.

* *True heroes are not afraid **of making tough choices**.*
    * **afraid:** Predicate Adjective.
    * **of making tough choices:** Prepositional Complement (PP containing a gerund phrase argument).
* *She is very good **at math**.*
    * **at math:** Prepositional Complement (PP explaining the exact domain of proficiency).

---

## 2. Infinitival Clause Complements

Infinitival complements express an action, intention, or emotional trigger tied to the adjective. These split into two major syntactic configurations:

### A. Standard Control Infinitives
The subject of the main sentence is also the active agent performing the action in the infinitive clause.
* *True heroes are not afraid **to make tough choices**.*
    * **to make tough choices:** Infinitival Adjective Complement (Explaining the action the subject is afraid to perform).

### B. Tough-Complements (Tough-Movement Construction)
> 💡 **Notebook Integration:** This occurs with evaluative adjectives like *easy, tough, hard, fun, pleasant*. Here, the main subject of the sentence has been "raised" or pulled out from the object position inside the infinitive clause, frequently leaving behind a **stranded preposition**.
* *You are fun **to be with**.*
    * **fun:** Functioning as a predicate attribute.
    * **to be with:** Non-Finite Infinitival Clause functioning as a Tough-Complement (Adjective Complement) with a stranded preposition.

---

## 3. Participle Phrase Complements (-Ing Phrases)

A select group of adjectives (*busy, worth, trapped*) possesses the unique lexical property of directly selecting a bare present participle phrase without requiring a prepositional connector.

* *This tool is busy **drilling the hole**.*
    * **busy:** Predicate Adjective.
    * **drilling the hole:** Present Participle Phrase acting as an Adjective Complement (Detailing the exact activity occupying the tool).

---

## 4. Clausal Complements (Finite Content / That-Clauses)

Used after adjectives expressing mental states, certainties, or emotional conditions (*sure, glad, aware, certain, afraid*).

* *I am sure **that he will arrive**.*
    * **sure:** Predicate Adjective.
    * **that he will arrive:** Clausal Complement (Finite Content Clause explaining the fact or certainty).
    * **that:** Optional **Complementizer** signaling the clausal boundary.

---

### 💡 Syntactic Guardrail: Complement vs. Adjunct

To ensure an element is a true Adjective Complement rather than a Clausal Adjunct, apply the **Semantic Completeness Test**:

1. *Sentence A:* "I am glad **that you passed the test**."
    * If you delete the clause: *"I am glad."* The sentence is structurally sound, but the core semantic reason for being glad is entirely lost. This is a **Complement**.
2. *Sentence B:* "He is happy **because it is sunny**."
    * The phrase *because it is sunny* answers a circumstantial "why" using an adverbial connector. It modifies the entire predicate clause rather than completing the internal meaning of *happy*. This is an **Adverbial Adjunct**.
