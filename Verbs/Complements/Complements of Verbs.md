# Complement of Verbs

A complement of verbs consists of any and all structural elements that are syntactically mandatory for a verb to complete its meaning and form a grammatical sentence.

We have these primary types:
1. Direct Object
2. Verbal Complement
3. Adverbial Complement
4. Prepositional Complement (Prepositional Phrase)
5. Object Complement
6. Clausal Complement
7. Subject Complement
8. Measure Complement (Quasi-Objects)

---

# 1. Direct Object (Monotransitive Verbs)

The primary noun argument that is directly affected, selected, or acted upon by a **Monotransitive Verb**.

* *The workers **extracted** **the ore**.*
  * **extracted:** Monotransitive Verb.
  * **the ore:** Direct Object (Noun Phrase).

> ⚠️ **Terminology Reminder:** Avoid calling the direct object an "Object Complement." The Direct Object is an independent core argument; an Object Complement is a secondary piece that modifies or describes that object.

---

# 2. Verbal Complement

A non-finite clause or participle structure that completes a verb's meaning by describing a potential action, required process, or state.

## 2.1. With Intransitive Verbs (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause)
Completes an intransitive verb of choice, intention, or agreement.

* *I agreed **to attend**.*
  * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb.
  * **to attend:** Verbal Complement (Non-Finite To-Infinitive Clause). Its implicit subject (agent) is *I*.

### 2.1.1. Overt Agents (The Complementizer "For" Structure)
Verbs of choice or agreement can allow the infinitive clause to have its own distinct subject. In this case, "for" is not acting as a true preposition, but as a structural marker.

* *I agreed **for him to attend**.*
  * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb.
  
  * **for him to attend:** Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause). 
  
  * **for:** Functions as a **Complementizer**.
  
  * **him:** The overt subject (agent) of the infinitive verb *to attend*.
  
    

## 2.2. With Transitive Verbs (Complex Transitive Verbs - CTVs)
In these structures, a **Complex Transitive Verb (CTV)** takes a Direct Object followed by a non-finite phrase acting as an Object Complement.

### 2.2.1. Infinitive and Bare Infinitive (Shows completed action or intent)
* *He made me **clean my room**.* (me = DO; *clean my room* = Bare Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I saw the sun **set**.* (the sun = DO; *set* = Bare Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I want my father **to go** there.* (my father = DO; *to go there* = To-Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I get my brother **to clean my room**.* (my brother = DO; *to clean my room* = To-Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)

### 2.2.2. Participles (Aspectual & Voice Variations)
* **Present Participle (Ongoing Action):** *I watched the sun **setting**.*
  * *watched* is a CTV (Verb of Perception).
  * *the sun* is the Direct Object (NP).
  * *setting* is the Object Complement (Present Participle describing an ongoing, incomplete process).
* **Past Participle (Passive Result State):** *I saw my room **cleaned**.*
  * *cleaned* is the Object Complement (Past Participle showing a passive resultative state). The relationship between the DO (*my room*) and the complement is passive.
* **Resultative Get/Have Structure:** *It gets the job **done**.* / *I had my room **cleaned**.*
  * Structure: `Subject + Get/Have + Object + Past Participle = Result`.

> 💡 **Comparison Checklist:**
> * **Verbs of Perception (Watch/See/Hear):** Highly flexible. Can take Bare Infinitives (completed), Present Participles (ongoing), or Past Participles (passive).
> * **Causative Verbs (Make, Have):** Can take Bare Infinitives or Past Participles, but generally reject Present Participles.

---

# 3. Adverbial Complement

A locative, temporal, or directional phrase that a verb strictly requires to complete its structural frame. Without it, the sentence crashes.

## 3.1. With Intransitive Verbs (Complex Intransitive Verbs - CIVs)
When an intransitive verb requires an adverbial phrase, it is classified as a **Complex Intransitive Verb** or a **Copular-Like** verb.

### A. Place and Time (Locative / Temporal)
* *The deposits **reside** **underground**.*
  * **reside:** CIV / Locative Verb.
  * **underground:** Locative Adverbial Complement (Adverb of Place).
* *The shift **lasted** **until midnight**.*
  * **lasted:** CIV / Duration Verb.
  * **until midnight:** Temporal Adverbial Complement (Prepositional Phrase acting as an Adverb of Time).

### B. Result, Manner, or State (Transformational)
* *The food **turned** **into poison**.*
  * **turned:** CIV / Copular-like aspectual verb.
  * **into poison:** Adverbial Complement (PP representing the final result of the transformation).

## 3.2. With Transitive Verbs (Complex Transitive Verbs - CTVs)
* *They **put** the book **on the table**.*
  * **put:** CTV (Requires both a DO and a spatial location).
  * **the book:** Direct Object (NP).
  * **on the table:** Locative Adverbial Complement (PP).

> ⚠️ **Note:** An Adverbial Complement is **not** a type of Object Complement. An Object Complement characterizes the internal qualities, state, or identity of the object itself; an Adverbial Complement simply places that object into an external, physical context.

---

# 4. Prepositional Complement (Prepositional Phrase)

A complement introduced by a mandatory, syntactically fixed prepositional "hook" tied directly to the verb.

## 4.1. With Intransitive Verbs (Prepositional Verbs)
* *I **listened** **to the book**.*
  * **listened:** Prepositional Verb.
  * **to the book:** Prepositional Complement (PP).
* *I **agreed** **to it**.*
  * **agreed:** Prepositional Verb.
  * **to it:** Prepositional Complement (PP).

> 💡 **Notebook Tip (Infinitive-Only Verbs):** Verbs like *agree, refuse, and promise* reject gerund complements (*"I agreed to attending"* is incorrect) and demand a to-infinitive for actions. Contrast this with idiomatic structures where "to" is a true preposition that naturally accepts a gerund phrase: *look forward to, be used to, object to, commit to*.

## 4.2. With Transitive Verbs (Transitive Prepositional / Prepositional Ditransitives)
Occurs when a transitive verb maps an extra participant argument through a mandatory prepositional phrase alongside a direct object.

* *I **provided** him **with the file**.*
  * **provided:** Transitive Prepositional Verb.
  * **him:** Direct Object.
  * **with the file:** Prepositional Complement / Oblique Object (PP).

> ⚠️ **Note:** The Prepositional Complement in this structure is **not** an Object Complement.

---

# 5. Object Complement (Complex Transitive Verbs - CTVs)

The core word or phrase that directly attributes a *state, result, mental label, or identity* to the Direct Object.

## 5.1. Adjective Phrase
* **Resultative Adjective (Object = New State):** *He made the bread **brown**.* (The bread changed to a brown state because of the action).
* **Depictive Adjective (Object + Simultaneous State):** *I found it **interesting**.* (It was interesting while I evaluated/experienced it).
* **Maintained State (Required Condition):** *I had my room **clean**.* (Describing a mandatory ongoing state or condition you required).

## 5.2. Noun Phrase (Object = Identity/Role)
A noun phrase that assigns an official role, identity, or title to the direct object.
* *They elected him **manager**.*
  * **him:** Direct Object.
  * **manager:** Object Complement (NP establishing the object's new identity).

---

# 6. Clausal Complement (Finite Content / That-Clause)

A finite clause acting as a core complement, typically introduced by a complementizer.

* *I agreed **that I would attend**.*
  * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb taking a clausal complement.
  * **that:** Optional **Complementizer** (Subordinating Conjunction) signaling the clausal boundary. It does not replace a noun phrase.

---

# 7. Subject Complement (Copular / Linking Verbs)

A word or phrase that co-refers to and describes the permanent or temporary state, condition, or identity of the subject.

* *The river is **solid**.*
  * **is:** Copular (Linking) Verb.
  * **solid:** Subject Complement (Adjective Phrase).

---

# 8. Measure Complement (Quasi-Objects)

A **Measure Complement** is a unique structural element that behaves as an *obligatory scalar boundary* (answering "how much/how long" rather than "what"). They are structurally Noun Phrases (NPs) or Prepositional Phrases (PPs) but fail the classic passive voice transformation test.

### Group 1: Intransitive Measure Complements (CIVs)
Paired with scalar intransitive verbs where the subject undergoes the measurement.
* *The car **costs** **ten thousand dollars**.*
  * **costs:** Measure Verb (CIV).
  * **ten thousand dollars:** Measure Complement (NP). *(Passive fails: \* "Ten thousand dollars is costed by the car.")*
* *The gold **weighs** **ten pounds**.*

### Group 2: Transitive Measure Complements (Monotransitive + Measure)
Paired with transitive verbs of consumption, distance, or time duration where the phrase occupies a structural direct object slot.
* *The journey **took** **three hours**.*
  * **took:** Transitive Verb.
  * **three hours:** Transitive Measure Complement (NP). *(Passive fails: \* "Three hours was taken by the journey.")*

### Group 3: Ditransitive Measure Complements (Double Object + Measure)
Occurs when measure or time verbs allow an extra argument to indicate the person experiencing or paying the measurement.
* *The ticket **cost** **me** **fifty dollars**.*
  * **me:** Indirect Object.
  * **fifty dollars:** Measure Complement (NP).
* *The flight **took** **us** **twelve hours**.*
* *The mistake **charged** **him** **his life**.*

### Group 4: Prepositional Measure Complements (Monotransitive + PP Measure)
Occurs with transitive measurement verbs (*time, clock, rate*) where the final scalar value is introduced via a mandatory prepositional phrase.
* *He **timed** the process **at three hours**.*
  * **timed:** Monotransitive Verb.
  * **the process:** Direct Object (NP).
  * **at three hours:** Adverbial Complement of Measure (PP).
* *The police **clocked** the car **at ninety miles per hour**.*
