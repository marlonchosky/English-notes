# For Intransitive Verbs

## 1. Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause)
Completes an intransitive verb of choice, intention, or agreement.

* **Example A:** *I agreed **to attend**.*
    * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb.
    * **to attend:** Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause). Its implicit subject (agent) is *I*.
* **Example B:** *I agreed **for him to attend**.*
    * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb.
    * **for him to attend:** Verbal Complement (Non-Finite Infinitive Clause). *for* functions as the **Complementizer**, and *him* is the overt subject (agent) of the infinitive verb.



## 2. Adverbial Complement (Complex Intransitive Verbs)

An **Adverbial Complement** is a phrase that functions like an adverb but is *syntactically obligatory* for the sentence to be complete. Verbs that require these are called **Complex Intransitive Verbs (CIVs)**.

### A. Place and Time (Locative / Temporal)
* *The deposits **reside** **underground**.*
    * **reside:** CIV / Locative Verb.
    * **underground:** Locative Adverbial Complement (Adverb of Place).
* *The shift **lasted** **until midnight**.*
    * **lasted:** CIV / Duration Verb.
    * **until midnight:** Temporal Adverbial Complement (Prepositional Phrase acting as an Adverb of Time).

### B. Result, Manner, or State (Transformational)
* *The food **turned** **into poison**.*
    * **turned:** CIV / Copular-like aspectual verb.
    * **into poison:** Adverbial Complement (PP representing the final result of the transformation).
        * *into:* Head word of the PP.
        * *poison:* Object of the preposition.



## 3. Prepositional Complement (Prepositional Verbs)

Intransitive verbs that require a specific prepositional "hook" to connect to their complement. The preposition is syntactically fixed to the verb.

* *I **listened** **to the book**.*
    * **listened:** Prepositional Verb.
    * **to the book:** Prepositional Complement (PP).
* *I **agreed** **to it**.*
    * **agreed:** Prepositional Verb.
    * **to it:** Prepositional Complement (PP).

> 💡 **Notebook Tip (Infinitive-Only Verbs):** Verbs like *agree, refuse, and promise* reject gerund complements (*"I agreed to attending"* is incorrect) and demand a to-infinitive for actions. Contrast this with idiomatic structures where "to" is a true preposition that naturally accepts a gerund phrase: *look forward to, be used to, object to, commit to*.



## 4. Clausal Complement (Finite Content / That-Clause)

* *I agreed **that I would attend**.*
    * **agreed:** Intransitive Verb taking a finite clausal complement.
    * **that:** Optional **Complementizer** (Subordinating Conjunction). It does not stand in for a noun; it simply signals the start of the clausal boundary.



## 5. Subject Complement (Copular / Linking Verbs)

Co-refers to and describes the permanent or temporary state/identity of the subject.
* *The river is **solid**.*
    * **is:** Copular (Linking) Verb.
    * **solid:** Subject Complement (Adjective Phrase).

# For Transitive Verbs

## 1. Direct Object (Monotransitive Verbs)
The primary noun argument that is directly affected, selected, or acted upon by a **Monotransitive Verb**.

* *The workers **extracted** **the ore**.*
    * **extracted:** Monotransitive Verb.
    * **the ore:** Direct Object (Noun Phrase).
    * *⚠️ Terminology Reminder:* Do not call the direct object an "Object Complement." The Direct Object is an independent core argument; an Object Complement is a secondary piece that modifies the object.



## 2. Verbal Complement (Complex Transitive Verbs)

A **Complex Transitive Verb (CTV)** takes a Direct Object followed by a non-finite clause or participle structure that serves as an **Object Complement**.

### A. Infinitive and Bare Infinitive (Shows completed action or intent)
* *He made me **clean my room**.* (me = DO; *clean my room* = Bare Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I saw the sun **set**.* (the sun = DO; *set* = Bare Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I want my father **to go** there.* (my father = DO; *to go there* = To-Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)
* *I get my brother **to clean my room**.* (my brother = DO; *to clean my room* = To-Infinitive Phrase acting as Object Complement)

### B. Participles (Aspectual & Voice Variations)
* **Present Participle (Ongoing Action):** *I watched the sun **setting**.*
    * *watched* is a CTV (Verb of Perception).
    * *the sun* is the Direct Object (NP).
    * *setting* is the Object Complement (Present Participle describing the ongoing, real-time action of the object).
* **Past Participle (Passive Result State):** *I saw my room **cleaned**.*
    * The structural relationship between the object (*my room*) and the complement (*cleaned*) is **passive**. It describes a completed state that happened to the object.
* **Resultative Get/Have Structure:** *It gets the job **done**.* / *I had my room **cleaned**.*
    * Structure: `Subject + Get/Have + Object + Past Participle = Result`.

> 💡 **Comparison Checklist:**
> * **Verbs of Perception (Watch/See/Hear):** Highly flexible. Can take Bare Infinitives (completed), Present Participles (ongoing), or Past Participles (passive).
> * **Causative Verbs (Make, Have):** Can take Bare Infinitives or Past Participles, but generally reject Present Participles.



## 3. Adverbial Complement (Complex Transitive Verbs)

A locative, spatial, or directional phrase that a transitive verb strictly requires to complement its direct object. Without it, the structural frame crashes.

* *They **put** the book **on the table**.*
    * **put:** CTV (Requires both a DO and a spatial location).
    * **the book:** Direct Object (NP).
    * **on the table:** Locative Adverbial Complement (PP).
    * *Note:* An Adverbial Complement is **not** an Object Complement. An Object Complement characterizes the internal qualities, state, or identity of the object itself; an Adverbial Complement simply places that object into a physical, external context.



## 4. Prepositional Complement (Transitive Prepositional / Ditransitive Verbs)

Occurs with verbs that map an argument through a mandatory prepositional phrase alongside a direct object.

* *I **provided** him **with the file**.*
    * **provided:** Transitive Prepositional Verb (or Prepositional Ditransitive).
    * **him:** Direct Object.
    * **with the file:** Prepositional Complement / Oblique Object (PP).
    * *Note:* This prepositional complement is **not** an Object Complement.



## 5. Object Complement (Complex Transitive Verbs)

The core word or phrase that directly attributes a *state, result, mental label, or identity* to the Direct Object.

### 5.1. Adjective Phrase
* **Resultative Adjective (Object = New State):** *He made the bread **brown**.* (The bread changed to a brown state because of the action).
* **Depictive Adjective (Object + Simultaneous State):** *I found it **interesting**.* (It was interesting while I evaluated/experienced it).
* **Maintained State (Required Condition):** *I had my room **clean**.* (Describing a mandatory ongoing state/condition you required).

### 5.2. Noun Phrase (Object = Identity/Role)
A noun phrase that assigns an official role, identity, or title to the direct object.
* *They elected him **manager**.*
    * **him:** Direct Object.
    * **manager:** Object Complement (NP establishing the object's new identity).



# The System of Measure Complements (Quasi-Objects)

A **Measure Complement** is a unique structural element that behaves as an *obligatory scalar boundary* (answering "how much" rather than "what"). They are structurally Noun Phrases (NPs) but fail the classic passive voice transformation test.

### Group 1: Intransitive Measure Complements (CIVs)

Paired with scalar intransitive verbs where the subject undergoes the measurement.

* *The car **costs** **ten thousand dollars**.*
  * **costs:** Measure Verb (CIV).
  * **ten thousand dollars:** Measure Complement (NP).
  * *(Note: Passive fails: \* "Ten thousand dollars is costed by the car.")*
* *The gold **weighs** **ten pounds**.*

### Group 2: Transitive Measure Complements (Monotransitive + Measure)

Paired with transitive verbs of consumption, distance, or time duration.

* *The journey **took** **three hours**.*
  * **took:** Transitive Verb.
  * **three hours:** Measure Complement (NP).
  * *(Note: Passive fails: \* "Three hours was taken by the journey.")*

### Group 3: Ditransitive Measure Complements (Double Object + Measure)

Occurs when measure verbs or time verbs allow an extra argument to indicate the person experiencing or paying the measurement.

* *The ticket **cost** **me** **fifty dollars**.*
  * **me:** Indirect Object.
  * **fifty dollars:** Measure Complement (NP).
* *The flight **took** **us** **twelve hours**.*
* *The mistake **charged** **him** **his life**.*

### Group 4: Prepositional Measure Complements (Monotransitive + PP Measure)
Occurs with transitive measurement verbs (like *time, clock, rate*) where the final scalar measurement is introduced via a mandatory prepositional phrase.

* *He **timed** the process **at three hours**.*
    * **timed:** Monotransitive Verb.
    * **the process:** Direct Object (NP).
    * **at three hours:** Adverbial Complement of Measure (PP). It provides the obligatory value resulting from the action of timing.
* *The police **clocked** the car **at ninety miles per hour**.*
