# Verbal Adjuncts (Clausal Adjuncts)

An **Adjunct** is any structural element within a sentence that is grammatically **optional**. You can completely delete an adjunct, and the remaining sentence remains fully grammatical and structurally sound.

We categorize verbal adjuncts into three main structural types:
1. Adverbial Adjuncts
2. Secondary Predicate Adjuncts (Predicative Adjuncts)
3. Prepositional Adjuncts

---

# 1. Adverbial Adjuncts

These modify the core verb phrase by providing background context about **how**, **when**, or **where** the action happened.

* *The river froze **quickly**.* (Adverb of Manner)
* *The river froze **at night**.* (Prepositional Phrase acting as an Adverb of Time)
* *He ate the pizza **hungrily**.* (Adverb of Manner)

> ⚠️ **Notebook Cross-Reference:** Do *not* include sentences like *"He timed the process at three hours"* here. In that case, "at three hours" is a mandatory *Measure Complement*, not an optional adjunct.

---

# 2. Secondary Predicate Adjuncts (Predicative Adjuncts)

A **Secondary Predicate Adjunct** expresses an optional, secondary state of a noun (Subject or Object) while the main action occurs, or as a non-obligatory result. 

Because they contain an "invisible clause" (e.g., *The river [was] blue*), they function as secondary predicates. They can be **Depictive** or **Resultative**.

### A. Subject-Oriented Depictive Adjuncts
* *The river glowed **blue**.*
    * **Primary Predicate:** *The river glowed.* (Complete sentence)
    * **Secondary Predicate:** *The river [was] blue.*
    * **Form:** Adjective Phrase.
* *He died **a hero**.*
    * **Secondary Predicate:** *He [was] a hero.*
    * **Form:** Noun Phrase (NP).

### B. Object-Oriented Depictive Adjuncts
* *He ate the pizza **cold**.*
    * **Secondary Predicate:** *The pizza [was] cold.*
    * **Form:** Adjective Phrase modifying the Direct Object.

### C. Resultative Adjuncts
These express a final state that occurs *as a result* of the action, but the sentence does not structurally depend on them to be complete.
* *The river froze **solid**.*
    * **Primary Predicate:** *The river froze.* (Complete sentence)
    * **Secondary Predicate:** *The river became solid.*
    * **Form:** Adjective Phrase.
* *He wiped the table **to a shine**.*
    * **Secondary Predicate:** *The table became shiny.*
    * **Form:** Prepositional Phrase (PP).

> ⚠️ **Notebook Cross-Reference:** Move sentences like *"The vase broke into pieces"* to your Complements file. Physical fragmentation transformations with *break* or *melt* require **Prepositional Resultative Complements** to bound the verb.

---

# 3. Prepositional Adjuncts

These are structural Prepositional Phrases (PPs) that add optional beneficiary, instrumental, or situational arguments to the sentence.

* *He bought a car **for his brother**.*
    * **bought:** Monotransitive Verb.
    * **a car:** Direct Object (NP).
    * **for his brother:** Prepositional Adjunct (PP denoting the beneficiary; entirely optional to the core syntax of *buying*).
