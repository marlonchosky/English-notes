## 1. Ergative Verbs (Alternating Unaccusatives)

An **Ergative Verb** (also called a *labile* or *ambitransitive* verb) is a verb that can transition between a **Transitive (Causative)** structure and an **Intransitive (Unaccusative)** structure. 

The defining feature of an ergative verb is that the **Direct Object** of the transitive sentence becomes the **Subject** of the intransitive sentence without changing the underlying meaning of the action.

### The Causative-Inchoative Alternation

* **Transitive (Causative Form):** *The chef melted the butter.*
  * **Grammar Breakdown:** *The chef* is the Agent causing the action, *melted* is a Monotransitive Verb, and *the butter* is the Direct Object (Patient).
* **Intransitive (Unaccusative/Inchoative Form):** *The butter melted.*
  * **Grammar Breakdown:** *The butter* shifts to the Subject position. It is an **Unaccusative Verb** because the subject is not actively performing an action; it is spontaneously undergoing an internal change of state.

### Common Ergative Verbs:

* *break* ("He broke the vase" / "The vase broke")
* *open* ("She opened the door" / "The door opened")
* *freeze* ("The cold froze the lake" / "The lake froze")

## 2. Unergative Verbs

An **Unergative Verb** is a strictly intransitive verb where the subject is an **active, deliberate agent** who is in complete control of the action. 

* **The Core Logic:** `Subject = Agent (The Active Doer)`. 
* **Examples:** *He ran.* / *She laughed.* / *They shouted.*
* Unlike unaccusative subjects, an unergative subject is actively producing the event rather than passively undergoing a change of state.

### Expressing Results: The "Fake Object" Shift

Because unergative verbs are fundamentally intransitive and their subjects are agents, they cannot take a resultative adjective directly (*"He ran tired"* means he was tired *while* running → Depictive). 

To force a resultative meaning, English employs a **"Fake" (Reflexive) Object**. This syntactic trick converts the verb into a **Complex Transitive Verb (CTV)** frame, allowing a resultative complement to anchor onto the fake object and reflect back to the subject.

* **Structure:** `[Subject] + [Unergative Verb as CTV] + [Reflexive/Fake Object] + [Resultative Complement]`

#### Examples:

* *He ran **himself** **tired**.*
  * **ran:** Functions as a CTV.
  * **himself:** Fake Reflexive Object.
  * **tired:** Resultative Object Complement. (He is tired as a direct result of the running).
* *She cried **herself** **to sleep**.*
  * **to sleep:** Prepositional Resultative Complement (PP acting as a state/endpoint).
* *They shouted **themselves** **red in the face**.*
  * **red in the face:** Resultative Object Complement (Adjective Phrase showing final physical state).
