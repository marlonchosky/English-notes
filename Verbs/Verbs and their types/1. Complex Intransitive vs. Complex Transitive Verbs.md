# Complex Intransitive vs. Complex Transitive Verbs

The foundational difference between these two classes comes down to **argument mapping**: Complex Intransitive frames focus entirely on the **Subject**, whereas Complex Transitive frames focus on a **Direct Object**.

---

# 1. Complex Intransitive Verbs (CIVs) & Copular Verbs

This family deals entirely with assigning states, identities, boundaries, or locations to the **Subject**. Syntacticians split this domain into two distinct verb types based on what kind of complement they require:

## A. Copular / Linking Verbs (Require a Subject Complement)
These verbs act like an equals sign ($=$) or a transitional arrow (→). They do not express an action; they link the subject to a **Subject Complement** (Noun Phrase or Adjective Phrase) that defines its identity or state.

### 1. Verbs of Being/Identity (The Stative Equal Sign)
* **Verbs:** *be, become, remain, stay*
* **Example:** *He **became** a senior developer.* * **Blueprint:** `Subject [He] = Subject Complement [a senior developer]`

### 2. Verbs of Perception (The Sensory Appearance)
* **Verbs:** *seem, appear, look, sound, smell, feel, taste*
* **Example:** *The logic **sounds** correct.* * **Blueprint:** `Subject [The logic] = Subject Complement [correct]`

### 3. Verbs of Transition/Result (The Aspectual Change)
* **Verbs:** *grow, turn, get, fall*
* **Example:** *The weather **turned** cold.* * **Blueprint:** `Subject [The weather] → New State [cold]`

---

## B. True Complex Intransitive Verbs (Require an Adverbial or Measure Complement)
Unlike simple linking verbs, true **Complex Intransitive Verbs (CIVs)** require an **Adverbial Complement** (a locative/temporal phrase) or a **Measure Complement** to fulfill their baseline grammatical structure. If you remove these phrases, the sentence crashes.

* **Locative/Temporal Complements:**
    * *The deposits **reside** **underground**.* (`Subject + CIV + Locative Complement`)
    * *The shift **lasted** **until midnight**.* (`Subject + CIV + Temporal Complement`)
    * *The server **is** **in the rack**.* *(Note: Here, the copula "be" shifts from a linking verb to a CIV because it demands a location).*
* **Measure Complements:**
    * *The gold **weighs** **ten pounds**.* (`Subject + CIV + Measure Complement`)

---

# 2. Complex Transitive Verbs (CTVs)

**Core Blueprint:** `[Subject] + [Verb] + [Direct Object] + [Complement]`

A **Complex Transitive Verb** requires both a **Direct Object** and a secondary element (an **Object Complement** or an **Adverbial Complement**) that characterizes, locates, or details the state/action of that object.

## A. With Adverbial Complements (Spatial/Locative)
The complement places the direct object into an external, mandatory physical position.
* *They **put** the book **on the table**.* * **Blueprint:** `Subject [They] + CTV [put] + DO [the book] + Locative Complement [on the table]`

## B. With Object Complements (States, Identities, & Actions)
The complement directly characterizes the internal qualities, final states, or actions of the direct object.

### 1. Adjective Phrase (Resultative)
* *I **made** him **brave**.* (His brave state came into existence *as a result* of my actions).

### 2. Adjective Phrase (Depictive / Maintained State)
* *I **had** the room **clean**.* (Describing an obligatory, ongoing condition expected of the object).

### 3. Noun Phrase (Identity/Role Attribution)
* *They **elected** him **leader**.* (Assigning a brand new identity/title to the direct object).

### 4. Infinitive Phrase (Bare or To-Infinitive Actions)
* *I **want** him **to go**.* / *I **made** him **go**.* (An obligatory future or forced action imposed onto the direct object).

### 5. Participle Phrase (Present or Past Aspect)
* *I **saw** it **moving**.* (Present participle showing an ongoing, real-time action of the direct object).
* *I **had** it **done**.* (Past participle showing a completed, passive result applied to the direct object).
