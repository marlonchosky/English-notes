# Indefinite Pronouns and Quantifiers: Syntax and Distribution

**Indefinite Pronouns** and **Indefinite Quantifiers** are functional words used to refer to entities or amounts without identifying specific individuals, exact numbers, or precise bounded boundaries.

---

## 1. The Functional Split: Pronoun vs. Quantifier (Determiner)

Many indefinite words shift dynamically between two structural roles depending on whether an overt noun head is present:

* **Indefinite Quantifier (Determiner Role):** Modifies an overt head noun within a Determiner Phrase (DP).
* **Indefinite Pronoun (Pronominal Role):** Operates as the sole structural Head of an NP, replacing the noun phrase entirely or standing in via ellipsis.

| Word        | Functional Role as Quantifier / Determiner | Functional Role as Indefinite Pronoun    |
| :---------- | :----------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------- |
| **Some**    | *I bought **some apples**.*                | *I wanted apples, so I bought **some**.* |
| **All**     | ***All miners** must register.*            | ***All** were rescued from the site.*    |
| **Many**    | ***Many students** passed.*                | ***Many** failed the examination.*       |
| **Few**     | *He has **few friends**.*                  | ***Few** survived the incident.*         |
| **Neither** | ***Neither path** is clear.*               | *I tried two keys; **neither** worked.*  |

## 2. Compound Indefinite Pronouns (The "-body / -one / -thing / -where" Series)

Compound indefinite pronouns are formed by merging an indefinite determiner (*some-, any-, every-, no-*) with a nominal suffix. They function **exclusively as pronouns** and can never act as determiners.

| Base       | Person (-body / -one)  | Thing (-thing) | Place (-where) |
| :--------- | :--------------------- | :------------- | :------------- |
| **Some-**  | *Somebody / Someone*   | *Something*    | *Somewhere*    |
| **Any-**   | *Anybody / Anyone*     | *Anything*     | *Anywhere*     |
| **Every-** | *Everybody / Everyone* | *Everything*   | *Everywhere*   |
| **No-**    | *Nobody / No one*      | *Nothing*      | *Nowhere*      |

### Key Syntactic Behaviors
1. **Strict Singular Agreement:** Even though *everyone* or *everybody* refers to a group, all compound indefinite pronouns are **grammatically singular** and require singular verb agreement:
   * *Everyone **is** ready.* (Not: *\*Everyone are ready*)
   * *Nobody **has** seen the report.*
2. **Post-Modification Structure:** Because compound pronouns are already complete lexical heads, adjectives **cannot pre-modify** them. Instead, adjectival modifiers must sit directly **after** the compound pronoun (Post-posed Adjectives):
   * *Incorrect:* *\*I need an interesting something.*
   * *Correct:* *I need **something interesting**.*
   * *Correct:* *There is **nobody available**.*

## 3. Assertive vs. Non-Assertive Domains (*Some* vs. *Any*)

Indefinite pronouns and quantifiers adhere to specific polarity domains:

### A. Assertive Domain (*Some- / Somebody / Something*)
* Typically restricted to **positive declarative sentences** where existence or reality is asserted.
  * *I found **some** data.* / *There is **someone** at the door.*
* **Polite Requests / Offers Exception:** Used in questions when a positive response is anticipated or solicited.
  * *Would you like **some** coffee?* / *Can **somebody** help me?*

### B. Non-Assertive Domain (*Any- / Anybody / Anything*)
* Typically restricted to **negative sentences** and **open questions** where existence is not asserted.
  * *I don't have **any** data.* (Negative)
  * *Is there **anybody** in the office?* (Question)
* **Free-Choice "Any" Exception:** When stressed in affirmative sentences, *any* acts as a free-choice quantifier meaning "it doesn't matter which one."
  * ***Anyone** can learn this syntax.* / *Pick **any** file you want.*

## 4. Opaque vs. Transparent Indefinite Pronouns

As established in your `Notional Agreement.md` and `Subject-Verb Agreement.md` master notes, indefinite pronouns split when triggering finite verb agreement in partitive (*of*-phrase) structures:

### A. Transparent Indefinite Pronouns (Look-Through Mechanism)
Indefinite pronouns like *all, some, most, half, none* lack inherent number features. In partitive structures, the finite verb **looks through** the pronoun to copy the number of the noun inside the PP:
* *Most of the **water is** gone.* (Mass Noun $\rightarrow$ Singular)
* *Most of the **miners are** safe.* (Plural Count Noun $\rightarrow$ Plural)

### B. Opaque Indefinite Pronouns (Strict Head Agreement)
Distributive indefinite pronouns like *each, either, neither, every one* act as **opaque structural heads**. They block look-through agreement and enforce singular verb agreement regardless of the plural noun inside the PP:
* *Each of the **miners is** assigned a shift.* (Enforces Singular Head Agreement)
* *Neither of the **options works**.* (Enforces Singular Head Agreement)
