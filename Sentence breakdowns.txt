### Other Verbs that use Past Participle Complements

It's not just for perception! "Causative" verbs and "Desire" verbs use this too:

- **Causative:** "I **had** the report **translated**."
- **Desire:** "I **want** the mineral samples **tested**."

In both cases, **"translated"** and **"tested"** are Object Complements wearing the **Past Participle** hat.

- ✅ **Past Participle Complements** always imply a **passive** meaning (the object was acted upon).

He agreed to attend.
* The verb agree has 3 primary "complementation patterns"
  -> 1. Agree + Infinitive Phrase (Non-finite complement)
     ---> He agreed to attend.
     ---> The infinitive phrase "to attend" is a Non-finite Complement (or Catenative Complement).
     ---> I will do this action
  -> 2. Agree to + Noun Phrase (Concept) (Inseparable Phrasal Verb)
     --> He agreed to it.
     --> This is an Inseparable Phrasal Verb (or Prepositional Verb).
     --> "Agreed to" acts as a single transitive unit. The "to" is a preposition that licenses a Noun Phrase or Pronoun as its object.
     --> I accept this thing
  -> 3. Agree + clause (Finite complement clause)
     --> He agreed [that] he would go
     --> Monotransitive verb, where the direct object is the full clause
     --> "that he would go": Finite Complement Clause (also called a Content Clause or Noun Clause).
         ----> "that": Complementizer (or Subordinating Conjunction). It marks the beginning of the clause
     --> I accept that this statement is true.
     --> Here, "agree" is functioning as a Reporting Verb, much like say, think or believe
         ----> In this structure, "that" is optional
* Agreed to + Gerund: Wrong
  -> Why? There's a concept called Economy of language: If a verb already has a dedicated structure to express an action (the Infinitive), it usually rejects the Gerund for that same meaning.
  -> The Infinitive ("Agree to attend"): This is the standard "action" complement. It focuses on the intent to perform the act.
  -> The Gerund ("Agree to attending"): Since the infinitive already "owns" the action slot, the gerund feels redundant and "clunky" to a native speaker.
  -> The Rule of Thumb: If a verb can take a to-infinitive, it rarely takes a preposition + gerund for the same meaning.
  -> The Rare Exception (When it DOES work)
    --> You can say "agree to attending" if you are treating "attending" not just as an action, but as a concept or a condition of a contract.
    --> Example: "He agreed to attending the classes every day as a condition of his scholarship."
    --> In this rare case, "attending the classes" is a Gerund Phrase acting as a Prepositional Object. The "to" is behaving as a preposition because it's pointing to a "requirement" or "condition" rather than just a simple action.
* "Agree to" vs. "Look forward to"
  -> The reason you must use a gerund with "look forward to" is that "look forward to" cannot take an infinitive. It only exists as a prepositional phrase.

Agreed that our team is better.
* Subject is omitted. Elliptical Noun Phrase

He agreed with me.
* Agreed with: Phrasal Verb, specifically a Prepositional verb
* I cannot say "I was agreed with". Why? the passive voice is usually reserved for objects that are affected by an action (e.g., "The ball was kicked").
  -> When you "agree to a plan," the status of the plan changes to "accepted."
  -> When you "agree with a person," the person remains unchanged. You are simply sharing a state of mind. Because the object ("me") isn't "acted upon" in a physical or transformational way, the passive voice feels ungrammatical.
* You likely see the passive "agreed" in technical or legal documentation, but notice the Subject is always a thing, never a person.

It was the last night of our vacation.
* "It": Ambient it
* "the last night of our vacation": Subject complement and NP
* "the": central determiner
* "last": post-determiner
* "of our vacation": prepositional phrase, post-modifier (adjunct) functioning adjectivally

The end of our vacation was sad.
* Different than the sentence before.
* The noun "end" is a Relational Noun. It doesn't point to an object, but to a limit or boundary of something else. Therefore, it licenses (requires) a complement to complete its meaning.
* "of our vacation": Noun complement and Prepositional Phrase
* Relational Nouns like end, beginning, middle, part, side, and top almost always take Noun Complements because they cannot exist without the thing they are a part of.
* In this context, the relation is a Partitive Genitive (specifically a Temporal Partitive).
  -> The "of-phrase" identifies the "whole" (our vacation), and the head noun "end" identifies a specific "part" or boundary of that whole. Because we are dealing with time rather than physical matter, it expresses a temporal limit.
  -> "Which specific segment of time are we talking about?"

The destruction of the city.
* "of the city": Noun Complement (specifically an Objective Genitive).
* Objective Genitive: Let's decode the noun back into its original verb form:
  -> [Someone] destroyed the city
  -> The city is the target of the destruction. It is being "acted upon". Therefore, "of the city" is an Objective Genitive
* Phrase: "The arrival of the train."
  -> Verb Test: "The train arrived."
  -> The train is the doer of the action. It is the one performing the arrival. Therefore, "of the train" is a Subjective Genitive.
* Why "of the city" is a complement.
  -> Just like with the word "end," "destruction" is a Transitive Noun.
  -> Because the verb "destroy" requires an object, the noun "destruction" requires a complement to feel complete.

The last dog of my parents' pets is nice.
* "The": Central Determiner
* "last": adjective post-determiner
* "dog": head word of the NP
* "of my parents' pets": Prepositional Phrase acting as Adjectival "of-Genitive" Modifier, showing a part-whole relationship.
* "my parents'": Possessive Genitive Determiner Phrase
* "pets": head word of the NP
* "my": possessive central determiner
* "parents'": Possessive Noun, the head of the possessive unit

The last 2 dogs of my parents' pets are nice. 
* "The": Central Determiner.
* "last": Ordinal Post-determiner 1. (Specifies the position in a sequence).
* "2": Cardinal Post-determiner 2 and Quantifier. (Specifies the exact count).
* dogs": Head Word (Plural Noun).
* "of my parents' pets": Prepositional Phrase acting as Adjectival "of-Genitive" Modifier. It describes the group from which the "2 dogs" are selected.

My parents' last pet dog is nice.
* "My parents'": Possessive Genitive Determiner Phrase.
* "my": Possessive Determiner
* "parents'": Possessive Noun. It is the head of the unit that "owns" the rest of the NP.
* "pet": Noun acting as an adjective (specifically called a Attributive Noun).
  -> Unlike a true adjective (like "brown"), an Attributive Noun cannot usually be made comparative. You wouldn't say "a petter dog."
  -> Its job is to categorize the head noun. A "pet dog" is a specific category of dog, just like a "race car" or a "software engineer."
* "dog": Head Word of the Subject NP.
