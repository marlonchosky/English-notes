# The Core Rule

The verb must match the **head noun** of the Subject NP in number (singular or plural), regardless of the phrases that follow it.

- The last dog of my parents' pets is nice
  - Head Noun: *dog*
    Number: singular
    Verb: is (singular)

- The last 2 dogs of my parents' pets are nice
  - Head Noun: *dogs*
    Number: Plural
    Verb: are (Plural)

# The Proximity Trap

A common mistake is "attraction" or "proximity agreement", where a writer accidentally matches the verb to the noun closest to it  (the *of-phrase*) rather than the head noun.

- The **quality** of the apples **are** good (<span type="incorrect">✕</span> *incorrect*)
- The **quality** of the apples **is** good (correct)

# Exceptions: Quantifiers and Fractions

When the subject uses words that indicate a portion or quantity, the verb often agrees with the noun in the *of-phrase* rather than the quantifier itself. This is because these quantifiers are seen as *transparent* or *headless*.

Common quantifiers that follow this exceptions are:

- Some of...
- All of...
- Most of...
- A lot of... / Lots of...
- Half of... (and other fractions/percentages)
- The rest of...
- None of...

In these cases before, the verb looks *through* the quantifier to the object of the preposition.

- Most of the **cake** **is** gone.
- Most of the **cookies** **are** gone.
- Half of the **water** **has** spilled.
- Half of the **students** **have** left
- None of the water **is** left.
- None of the students **are** here.

## Special cases

"**A number of**" and "**The number of**" are special cases:

- A number of
  - **Form**: A number of + Plural noun + **Plural Verb**
  - **Description**: It always takes a plural verb. It acts like a quantifier meaning "*many*".
  - **Example**: A number of students **are** waiting outside. 
- The number of
  - **Form**: The number of + Plural Noun + **Singular Verb**
  - **Description**: It always takes a singular verb. The head noun is literally the word "*number*" (the specific count)
  - **Example**: The number of students **is** increasing.

## Quantifiers as Determiners

When you use a quantifier as a determiner (as in *some students*) the rule to follow is [The Core Rule](#The Core Rule), so the verb must agree directly with that noun.

- Any information **is** helpful / Any updates **are** welcome.
- All music **is** art / All musicians **are** artists
- No time **is** left / No tickets **are** available.
- Most advice **is** good / Most people **are** kind.

Whether you use quantifiers as pronouns or determiners, they are *transparent*, so the verb looks through them to the noun or to the *of-phrase*, in order to determine the number of the noun. 

## Quantifiers as Pronouns

If the quantifier is a pronoun and there is no *of-phrase* after it,  the verb agreement still follows the noun *hidden* or *understood*.

- The coffee is hot. Some **is** spilling. (*some coffee*)
- The students are late. Some **are** here (*some students*)
- The books are old. Many **are** falling apart (*many books*)
- Is there any water? No, there **isn't** any (*any water*)
- The workers are tired. Most **are** taking a break. (*most of the workers*)
- The information is on the internet. Most **is** unreliable though. (*most of the information*)
  - <u>Note</u>: You can say "*Most information*" or "*Most of the information*" but they have different meanings. The first one is for a general statement; meanwhile, the second one is for something specific. 
    But saying "*Most of information*" is **wrong**. The rule is you have to use a determiner like *the, this, that, my,* or *your* before the noun.

## "No": The Quantifier, that is a determiner but can't be a pronoun

"*No*" is a quantifier because it provides information about quantity (specifically, a zero quantity).

"*No*" can be a determiner:

- No water **is**... (*uncountable*)
- No student **is**... (*singular countable*)
- No students **are**... (*plural countable*)

"*No*" cannot stand alone as a *pronoun*. If you want to use it as a pronoun, you must change the word to "**None**".

### "No" with singular countable nouns

While we often use plural nouns with "*no*" (like *No students are*), you can also use it with a singular countable noun **for emphasis**.

- No student is allowed to leave (This is more emphatic and formal than "*No students are*...")

## "None" (The "formal vs informal" divide)

"*None*" is a unique case that often confuses even native speakers.

- Strict/Formal rule

  "*None*" historically means "*not one*", so it should always be singular..

  - The students are here, but none is prepared

- Modern/Common usage

  In everyday English, "*none*" follows the **"hidden noun" rule** we just discussed.

  - The students are here, but none are prepared. (this is much more common and widely accepted today).



# Distributives

Distributes are a specific class of determiners and pronouns that refer to the individual members of a group, rather than the group as a whole.
Think of them like a *filter* that takes a crowd of people and forces you to look at them one person at a time. Because they focus on the individual, they almost always require singular verbs.
There are 4 primary distributives in English: **Each, Every, Either and Neither**

<u>Note</u>: Distributes are the head noun of the phrase, because they carry number. Even when they go with *of-phrases*, they are the head of the noun. This is different to normal quantifiers like *all, some, most*, etc. Think of distributives as *thick* or *opaque* quantifiers

## Each

Each is used to refer to individual members of a **group of two or more**.

**Function**: It can be a determiner (Each book) or a Pronoun (Each of the books).

**Rule**: Always followed by a singular countable noun and a singular verb.

**Examples**:

- Each student **has** a unique ID. Each **has** a single book too.
- Each of the students **is** responsible. (with an *of-phrase*)

### Exception

There is one rare case where "*each*" doesn't control the verb: when it comes after a plural pronoun. This is called "**appositive each**". This is to emphasize that the action applies to everyone individually. In this case, the plural subject is  the *boss* of the verb.

**Examples**:

- The students each have a book. (*"students" is the subject, so the verb is plural*)
- They each want a prize. (*they is the subject*)
- We each have a different opinion. (*we is the subject*)

## Every

Every is very similar to *each*, but it is used for **groups of three or more**.
While *each* focuses on the individual, *every* sees the individuals as a way to describe the whole group.

**Function**: Strictly a determiner. It cannot stand alone as a pronoun.

**Rule**: Always followed by a singular countable noun and a singular verb.

**Examples**:

- Every house on this street is white.

### Usage as a pronoun

If you want to use it like a pronoun, there are <u>3 primary ways</u> to do it:

1. **The Compound Pronouns (Everyone, Everybody, Everything)**

   The most common way. You combine *every* with a noun-like ending to create a single, standalone word.

   - **Everyone / Everybody**: Used for people
   - **Everything**: Used for objects or abstract concepts.

   **The verb rule**: Even though these words refer to a large group of  people or things, they are **grammatically singular**

   **Examples**:

   - Everyone **is** here.
   - Everything **was** lost.

2. **"Every one" (Two words)**

   This is often used when you are referring to a specific group that was previously mentioned. It is more emphatic and "*pointy*" than the compound words.

   **Structure**: Every one + of + [Plural noun/pronoun]

   **Example**: 

   - I bought ten books. *Every one of them* **is** a masterpiece.

   <u>Note</u>: Spelling:

   - **Everyone** (*one word*) = all the people
   - **Every one** (*two words*) = each individual item or person.

3. **Using "Each" as the Substitute**

   The most natural "fix" is usually to switch to its twin, *each*

   **Examples**

   - I have many tools and I use every (<span type="incorrect">✕</span> *incorrect*)
   - I have many tools and I use **each**. (*correct*)
   - I have many tools and I use **every one** (*correct*)

   In all these uses, **the verb is singular**.

   - Every one of the students **is** studying. (*the verb matches one, not "students"*)

## Either

Either is used when referring to a choice or a situation involving exactly 2 people or things. It means "one or the other"

**Function**: Determiner or Pronoun

**Rule**: Always singular

**Examples**:

- Either day **is** fine for me. (*Monday or Tuesday*)
- There are 2 chairs; either is comfortable.
- Either of the candidates is qualified. (*even though there are 2 candidates, we are talking about them one at a time*)

## Neither

Neither is the negative version of *either*. It means "**not the one and not the other**" of two people or things.

**Function**: Determiner or Pronoun.

**Rule**: Always singular.

**Examples**:

- Neither team has scored a goal.
- I tried 2 keys, but neither works.
- Neither of my parents speaks English. (*with an of-phrase*)

Now, there are special uses for Either and Neither:

## "Either... or" and "Neither... nor"

In these both cases, the rules for verb agreement shift away from the "*always singular*" rule of distributes. Instead, we use a rule called "**The Rule of Proximity**":
==The verb ignores the first subject and agrees only with the subject closes to it.==

**Examples**:

- Either the teacher or the students **are** ...
- Either the students or the teacher **is** ...
- Neither my cat nor my dogs **eat** ...
- Neither my dogs nor my cat **eats** ...

### Special Case: Different Pronouns (I, You, He/She)

When you use different persons, the proximity rule can sometimes sound a bit strange to the ear, but it remains grammatically correct.

**Examples**:

- Either you or I **am** going to clean this. (matches "*I*")
- Either I or you **are** going to clean this. (matches "*you*")
- Neither she nor they **are** ready. (matches "*they*")

<u>Note</u>: A Stylistic Tip: If a sentence sounds too awkward using the proximity rule, many writers choose to rephrase the sentence entirely: "*Either he is going to clean this, or I am*".



# None vs Either

Neither is for exactly two things, and none is for three or more.

- Neither is used exclusively for 2 people, objects, or ideas. It means "**not the one and not the other**".
- None is used for a group of three or more. It is the negative version of "all" or "some".

 

# Breakdown of sentences

**Example**: *Some cake **is** left*.

- When you say "Some cake is left", you are treating the cake as an *uncountable* mass or a *substance*.

- Meaning: A portion of the total amount of cake is left.

- Because you are treating it as a mass (uncountable), it is singular.

- Usage: This is very common when looking at a cake that has been partially eaten but is still sitting there as a single object or a general amount.

- *The countable Interpretation*:

  If you want to emphasize the specific individual slices or separate pieces, you must use the plural.

  - Some cakes **are** left. (*refers to multiple whole cakes*)
  - Some slices of cake **are** left. (*refers to the specific units*)

- *Other examples and interpretations*:

  - There **is** some pizza left. (*amount/mass*)
  - There **are** some pizzas left. (*multiple whole pizzas*)
  - There **are** some slices of pizza left. (*individual units*)

- Some + singular

  - With **uncountable nouns** is just normal usage. (Some **cake/water/money** → amount (*singular*))
  - With **countable nouns**, it means something unknown. (Some **student/guy/man** → unknown identity (*one random person*))
