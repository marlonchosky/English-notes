# Subject-Verb Agreement: Structural Mechanics & Agreement Domains

**Subject-Verb Agreement** (Formal Concord) requires a finite verb to copy the number features (singular or plural) of the **Structural Head Noun** within the Subject Determiner Phrase (DP), regardless of post-modifying phrases or intervening clauses.

---

## 1. The Core Rule: Structural Head Primacy

The finite verb agrees exclusively with the primary structural head noun of the subject, ignoring any nominals trapped inside post-modifying Prepositional Phrases (PPs) or Relative Clauses.

* *The last **dog** of my parents' pets **is** nice.*
  * **Head Noun:** *dog* (Singular) $\rightarrow$ **Verb:** *is* (Singular)
* *The last two **dogs** of my parents' pets **are** nice.*
  * **Head Noun:** *dogs* (Plural) $\rightarrow$ **Verb:** *are* (Plural)

### The Proximity Trap (Attraction Error)
A common error occurs when the verb mistakenly agrees with the nearest noun inside a post-modifier (the object of the preposition) rather than the true head noun.

* **Incorrect:** *\*The **quality** of the apples **are** good.*
* **Correct:** *The **quality** of the apples **is** good.*

---

## 2. Transparent Quantifiers: The "Look-Through" Agreement Domain

Certain indefinite quantifiers and fractions are syntactically **transparent** (or "headless"). Because the quantifier pronoun lacks inherent number features, the verb executes a **Look-Through Mechanism**, copying its agreement features directly from the noun inside the partitive *of*-phrase.

### Transparent Quantifier List
* *Some of...* / *All of...* / *Most of...*
* *A lot of...* / *Lots of...* / *The rest of...*
* *Half of...* (and all fractions / percentages)
* *None of...*

### Agreement Examples
* *Most of the **cake** **is** gone.* (Mass Noun $\rightarrow$ Singular Verb)
* *Most of the **cookies** **are** gone.* (Plural Count Noun $\rightarrow$ Plural Verb)
* *Half of the **water** **has** spilled.* (Mass Noun $\rightarrow$ Singular Verb)
* *Half of the **students** **have** left.* (Plural Count Noun $\rightarrow$ Plural Verb)

---

## 3. Special Quantifier Constructions

### A. "A Number of" vs. "The Number of"
* **A number of (+ Plural Noun):** Functions as a transparent phrasal quantifier meaning *"many"*. Triggers a **Plural Verb**.
  * *A number of students **are** waiting outside.*
* **The number of (+ Plural Noun):** The word **number** acts as a literal, opaque head noun denoting a specific numerical count. Triggers a **Singular Verb**.
  * *The number of students **is** increasing.*

### B. Quantifiers as Determiners vs. Elliptical Pronouns
* **As Determiners:** When a quantifier directly specifies a noun without *of*, standard head agreement applies (*Any information **is** helpful* / *Any updates **are** welcome*).
* **As Pronouns (Ellipsis):** When the head noun is omitted, the verb agrees with the **covert (understood) noun**:
  * *The coffee is hot. Some **is** spilling.* (*some [coffee]*)
  * *The students are late. Some **are** here.* (*some [students]*)

> ⚠️ **Determiner Guardrail:** You can say *"Most information"* (general) or *"Most of the information"* (specific), but *\*"Most of information"* is ungrammatical. A central determiner (*the, this, my*) is required after *most of* when followed by a noun.

### C. "No" vs. "None"
* **No (Pure Determiner):** Cannot function as a standalone pronoun. It quantifies a following noun directly:
  * *No water **is** left.* (Mass $\rightarrow$ Singular)
  * *No student **is** allowed to leave.* (Singular Count $\rightarrow$ Emphatic Singular)
  * *No students **are** allowed to leave.* (Plural Count $\rightarrow$ Plural)
* **None (Pronoun / Partitive Head):** Functions as the pronominal equivalent of *no*. 
  * **Formal / Historical Rule:** Treated as strictly singular (*"not one"*): *None of them **is** ready.*
  * **Modern / Standard Usage:** Follows Notional Concord with the hidden/partitive noun: *None of the students **are** ready.*

---

## 4. Distributives: Opaque Quantifiers

Unlike transparent quantifiers, **Distributives** (*Each, Every, Either, Neither*) are syntactically **opaque**. They force the speaker to evaluate items individually, acting as strict **singular structural heads** that enforce singular verb agreement—even when followed by a plural *of*-phrase.

### A. Each
* **Domain:** Individual members of a group of **two or more**.
* **Functions:** Determiner (*Each student*) or Pronoun (*Each of the students*).
* **Agreement:** Always **Singular**.
  * *Each student **has** an ID.*
  * *Each of the candidates **is** qualified.*
* **Exception (Appositive Each):** When *each* follows a plural subject pronoun, the plural pronoun remains the structural head:
  * *The students **each have** a book.* / *They **each want** a prize.*

### B. Every
* **Domain:** Individual members of a group of **three or more**, viewed collectively.
* **Functions:** Pure **Determiner** only (cannot stand alone as a pronoun).
* **Agreement:** Always **Singular** (*Every house on this street **is** white*).
* **Pronominal Substitutes:**
  1. *Everyone / Everybody / Everything* $\rightarrow$ Grammatically singular (*Everyone **is** here*).
  2. *Every one of [Plural NP]* $\rightarrow$ Emphatic partitive (*Every one of the books **is** a masterpiece*).
  3. *Each* $\rightarrow$ Direct pronoun substitute (*I have many tools and I use **each***).

### C. Either & Neither
* **Domain:** Exactly **two** entities.
  * **Either:** "One or the other" $\rightarrow$ Singular (*Either option **is** acceptable*).
  * **Neither:** "Not the one nor the other" $\rightarrow$ Singular (*Neither of my parents **speaks** English*).

---

## 5. Correlative Conjunctions: The Principle of Proximity

When subjects are connected by the correlatives **Either... or** or **Neither... nor**, standard head-noun agreement is suspended. Agreement is governed by the **Principle of Proximity**: the verb matches the person and number of the **closest subject**.

* *Either the manager or the **employees** **are** responsible.* (Matches plural *employees*)
* *Either the employees or the **manager** **is** responsible.* (Matches singular *manager*)
* *Neither she nor **they** **are** ready.* (Matches plural *they*)
* *Either you or **I am** going to clear this.* (Matches 1st person singular *I*)

---

## 6. Semantic Coercion: "Some" + Singular Count Nouns

The quantifier *some* exhibits two distinct semantic behaviors depending on noun class:

1. **Some + Uncountable Mass Noun / Plural Count Noun:** Standard quantification (portion/amount).
   * *Some **cake is** left.* (Portion of a single mass/substance)
   * *Some **cakes are** left.* (Multiple whole units)
2. **Some + Singular Countable Noun:** Functions as an **Indefinite Epistemic Modifier**, signaling an unknown, unspecified, or arbitrary individual entity.
   * *Some **guy is** waiting for you outside.* ($\rightarrow$ One specific person whose identity is unknown to the speaker).
